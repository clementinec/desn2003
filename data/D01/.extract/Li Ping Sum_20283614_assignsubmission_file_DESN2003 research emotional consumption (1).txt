FOMO and Nostalgia-Based Emotional Branding Strategies: Driving Emotional
                   Consumption Across Generation Z


   1.​ Abstract


This study examines how FOMO (Fear of Missing Out) and nostalgia-based
emotional branding strategies drive emotional consumption among Generation Z
(Gen Z) across fashion, beauty, and technology brands. In the digital age, Gen Z
consumers increasingly prioritize purchases that fulfill emotional needs—such as
identity construction, community belonging, and alignment with personal
values—over purely functional attributes. These emotional consumption patterns
create a bidirectional relationship with brand strategies, where Gen Z preferences
shape marketing tactics while adapted campaigns further intensify consumption
cycles.

A multi-case study methodology will be used, the research analyzes campaigns from
leading Gen Z brands (fashion: Supreme, Nike; beauty: CeraVe, Rare Beauty;
technology: Razer, Logitech) through document analysis of social media content,
limited-drop announcements, influencer partnerships, and performance metrics.
FOMO strategies—characterized by scarcity timers, flash sales, and urgency
messaging—target younger Gen Z seeking peer approval, while nostalgia tactics
leveraging Y2K aesthetics and retro authenticity appeal to older Gen Z pursuing
stability and cultural connection.

Findings reveal distinct emotional pathways: FOMO accelerates impulse buying
through anxiety-relief cycles, while nostalgia fosters attachment via psychological
security. Both mechanisms demonstrate self-reinforcing feedback loops where brand
adaptations amplify Gen Z's emotional consumption intensity.

This study advances emotional branding research by showing how strategies differ
across Gen Z life stages and how they directly influence consumption patterns. For
brand managers, it identifies opportunities for targeted emotional marketing across
product categories while raising ethical concerns about FOMO tactics. The findings
provide a foundation for understanding emotional consumption among digital-native
generations.
   2.​ Literature Review


2.1 Emotional Consumption Defintion

Emotional consumption refers to buying driven by emotional needs rather than
practical use, such as purchasing for pleasure, mood improvement, status
expression, or social connection (Puckhaber, 2025). It is closely aligned with hedonic
consumption, where consumers seek sensory pleasure, excitement, and
self-expression beyond basic functional needs (Zigu, 2011). It often involves
products used to manage or enhance mood (e.g. treats, fashion, entertainment) and
to construct identity or status, such as designer goods that provide pride and social
recognition. In stressful or negative states, hedonic choices can serve mood repair
by generating positive affect and emotional recovery (Balleyer & Fennis, 2022).

To define emotions in consumption, Richins develops the Consumption Emotion Sets
(CES) (see Table1), a validated taxonomy of 47 emotions experienced during
consumption (buying, using, owning products/services), organized into 16 clusters
including fear and contentment, which is relevant to FOMO and nostalgia (Richins,
1997). This framework underlines that consumption is embedded in rich emotional
episodes rather than simple satisfaction–dissatisfaction judgments.

Complementing this, Uses and Gratifications theory (U&G) posits that consumers
actively select products and media to fulfil emotional needs such as social
integration, excitement and tension releases (Doob, 1950). People use media and
symbolic goods to regulate arousal and mood: when bored or distressed, they seek
stimulating or comforting content to adjust their affective state (Doob, 1950).
Gratifications such as emotional release, entertainment and intrinsic enjoyment map
directly onto hedonic, emotionally driven consumption (e.g. streaming, gaming,
social media commerce). This framework suggests the reason why Gen Z seeks
emotionally charged consumption, but lacks integration with discrete emotion like
FOMO or nostalgia.


2.2 Gen Z Definition

Generation Z (born between 1997 and 2012) (Warren, 2024) represents the first
digital native generation, shaped by technology, social media, and global events like
the Great Recession and the pandemic.

2.3 Emotional Consumption in GenZ

2.3.1 FOMO in GenZ

Over 70% of Gen Z individuals report experiencing FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
regularly (Suryawanshi, 2025), with research showing significantly higher FOMO
levels (mean = 2.35) compared to Millennials (mean = 2.14), driven by their lifelong
digital connectivity and expectation of real-time social awareness (Chakrabarti,
2024).

Compressed trend cycles on platforms like TikTok mean missing a viral sound or
limited release equates to immediate social irrelevance within peer networks (Kalin
Anastasov, 2025). This characteristic environment fosters prevalent FOMO,
distinguishing Gen Z from previous generations.

2.3.2 FOMO as Driver of Emotional Consumption


FOMO triggers emotional consumption through a clear anxiety-relief mechanism.
Social media exposure creates fear of missing rewarding social experiences,
activating needs for belonging and peer approval (Przybylski et al., 2013). This
anxiety prompts hedonic purchases that temporarily alleviate exclusion fears while
signaling social status (Argan et al., 2022).
This pattern resembles compulsive behaviors, where buying provides immediate
gratification but increases future FOMO sensitivity, forming self-reinforcing
consumption cycles (Elhai et al., 2020). Studies confirm FOMO mediates the link
between social comparison and impulse buying (β=0.42, p<0.01), establishing it as a
key driver of Gen Z's emotional purchasing (Richins, 1997).


2.3.3 Nostalgia in Gen Z


Social media platforms like TikTok and Instagram keep bringing back old trends
through algorithms and user content. This creates "fauxstalgia" (second-hand
nostalgia) for Gen Z, who never lived through these times (InsightTrendsWorld,
2025). Hashtags like #Y2Kfashion and #2000sNostalgia gets millions of views with
nostalgic edits, thrift finds, and retro styles (low-rise jeans, butterfly clips, 2010s
filters) (Gupta, 2025).

2.3.4 Nostalgia as Driver of Emotional Consumption


TikTok's short videos create 2.5x more engagement than longer content. Y2K styles
regularly beat current trends (Emplicit, 2025). This format drives Gen Z to consume
and remix nostalgic elements for identity expression and escapism.

Unlike older generations who experienced the eras they reminisce about, Gen Z’s
relationship with nostalgia is vicarious. Their nostalgic experiences are shaped by
second-hand exposure through YouTube videos, Netflix reboots, curated Instagram
aesthetics, and TikTok throwback trends(Nations, 2022). Consequently, their
nostalgia is not rooted in lived experiences but in media-mediated representations of
the past. This makes them uniquely susceptible to retro-themed advertising that
invokes familiarity, security, and identity reinforcement in a fast-changing digital
environment. Studies suggest that Gen Z's affinity for retro media is not based on
lived experience but a stylized appreciation of the past, constructed through visual
storytelling and communal nostalgia (Alfasi & Weiss, 2022).

According to Wildschut et al. (2006), nostalgia enhances psychological well-being by
increasing social connectedness and existential meaning, which may influence
consumption behavior as a coping mechanism (Wildschut et al., 2006).
Thus, nostalgia acts not only as a promotional device but also as an emotional
support during identity development—a critical aspect for Gen Z(Nations, 2022).

2.4 Existing brand strategy

2.4.1 Gen Z's Top Brand Categories
Beauty, streetwear, sportswear, tech, and retail platforms dominate Gen Z
conversations. Recent analysis shows fashion, beauty, sportswear, and technology
categories make up over 70% of top-discussed brands, while traditional sectors like
cars and packaged goods represent less than 8% (Basis Signals, 2026).

2.4.2 Commonly Used Branding Strategies


2.4.2.1 Fashion and Sportswear: Influencers and Limited Drop


Fashion and sportswear brands mainly use influencer partnerships and limited
releases to match Gen Z's need for authenticity and urgency. Micro-influencers work
better than celebrities, especially for streetwear and athleisure brands like Gymshark
and Nike, where creator collaborations make up 47% of strategy focus (Opensend,
2025). Limited edition releases and flash sales create FOMO, as shown by
Supreme's weekly drops and Adidas Yeezy scarcity model (Opensend, 2025). At the
same time, nostalgia marketing with Y2K aesthetics and 90s sportswear relaunches
has become standard, with retro packaging gaining 43% higher TikTok engagement
than current content (Barton et al., 2025).


2.4.2.2 Beauty: Viral Challenges and Performance Positioning


Beauty brands rely on TikTok viral challenges and AR experiences as main
strategies, since 58% of Gen Z discover products through social media (BOF Team
& McKinsey & Company, 2023). Augmented Reality(AR) filters and #GRWM (Get
Ready With Me) videos promote brands like e.l.f. (Jarsking, 2025) and Rare Beauty
(lensblogadmin, 2023). Affordable performance positioning works well (CeraVe, The
Ordinary), with clinical effectiveness plus "clean girl" aesthetics (Sala, 2025).
User-generated content like before/after reviews provides social proof, while
personalisation through shade-matching quizzes and custom skincare routines
builds emotional attachment (Tolstoy, 2025).



2.4.2.3 Technology and Gaming: Community Building and Creator Integration
Tech brands focus on gaming culture integration and community platforms. Discord
servers, esports sponsorships, and beta tester programs create participatory brand
ecosystems (Barton et al., 2025). Gaming peripherals (Razer, Logitech) and mobile
brands partner with Twitch streamers and tech YouTubers for authentic
endorsements (Dmytro Murko, 2023) (Logitech, 2019), while limited edition hardware
drops follow fashion's FOMO model (Marketingdive, 2019). Visual storytelling
through unboxing videos and sleek product renders dominates Instagram and
YouTube strategies (Chaitanya Koneti et al., 2025).


2.4.3 Common Strategies Across Categories


Across all four categories, personalisation, social proof, and experiential marketing
form the core approach, supported by AI-driven recommendations and immersive
technologies. Recent studies confirm influencer credibility directly correlates with
Gen Z brand preferences in fashion, beauty, and tech sectors. marking a shift from
top-down messaging to peer-shaped narratives (Chaitanya Koneti et al., 2025). The
strategies reflect Gen Z's demand for immediacy, authenticity, and cultural relevance
through platform-native execution rather than interruptive advertising (Basis Signals,
2026a).



2.5 Research Gap



Existing literature largely describes how brands react to Gen Z preferences through
influencer marketing, scarcity tactics, and nostalgic design. However, it does not yet
clarify whether Gen Z’s emotional traits directly drive these strategic changes, nor
how these adjusted strategies further intensify Gen Z’s emotional consumption,
creating self‑reinforcing cycles of FOMO‑driven impulse buying and nostalgia‑based
brand dependence. The present study addresses both directions: how Gen Z
characteristics shape brand strategy, and how these strategies in turn amplify
emotional consumption across different Gen Z life stages.
