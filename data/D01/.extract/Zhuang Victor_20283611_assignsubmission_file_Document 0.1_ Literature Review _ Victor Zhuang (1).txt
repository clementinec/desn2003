Urgency and Early Preservation of Jiaohe Ancient City
Jiaohe Ancient City, also known as Yarkhoto, is an abandoned earthen vernacular city that once
served as a key node on the Silk Road, providing a site for research not only in the
socio-cultural histories of its surrounding region (Xie, 1998; Zhang, 2023) but also in
architectural and engineering wisdom (Zhang, 2023). Although the remains have stood for
thousands of years, their current condition remains concerning, encompassing the full spectrum
of deterioration in earthen structures (Li et al., 2011), noticeably caused by wind, sand, flooding,
and acidic rain (UNESCO Beijing Office, 1999; Richards et al., 2020). Understanding its
situation, preservation activities focusing on reinforcing and replicating started by the end of the
twentieth century, mainly relying on techniques such as ramming and material replacement (Xie,
1998; UNESCO Beijing Office, 1999). However, such traditional activities interfered with the site,
causing irreversible changes that may have transformed its architecture, and environmental
threats remained, only postponed.


Current Techniques of Earthen Architectural Heritage Site Conservation
Current methodologies in intervention build on suggestions for early preservation techniques
(UNESCO Beijing Office, 1999), especially the anchoring of weathered structures expected to
overturn (Li et al., 2011), by the updating and comparison of materials for strength with minimal
intervention (Ren et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). Anchoring materials, once
traditionally made of steel, were unable to withstand long-term exposure to the environment due
to the structure's metallic nature, leading to erosion (Ren et al., 2024). Novel methodologies,
such as compositing the steel structure for strength with bamboo and a resin coating (Ren et al.
2009), were comparatively better at targeting organic and problem-solving materials (Ren et al.
2024). Testing and comparisons of such anchoring methods were often conducted on-site (Li et
al., 2011; Lu et al., 2025). However, as pointed out by Yulan et al. (2023), excessive intervention
in arching existing structures harms the structural integrity of the architectural ruin, and with
anchors being mostly installed within the remains (Ren et al., 2024), placement of them
remains, in one sense, an endangering technique of intervention.
The Gap:
Noticing that the preservation techniques remained to be directly engaged with the remains,
which remained questionable as a solution due to possible structural integrity issues related to
the placement of the supports and the remaining direct physical contact with the
thousands-year-old site, responding to this issue, relevant research could be conducted for
other methods of preservation with indirect procressing, such as drilling or methodologies to
minimize harm from the installation of such anchoring stuctures using novel technologies in
detection and prediction.


Limitations of Current Review
The current review, as reflected in the reference list, is mainly composed of research articles
written by a single ethnicity and nationality. Alongside, possible articles of indirect yet
environmentally targeting solutions proposals could be discovered.
