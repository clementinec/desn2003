The Softness-Roughness Paradox 
In industrial automation, vacuum-based installing measures are optimized for non-porous substrates using high-power active systems. However, domestic application on painted drywall introduces a challenge from Surface Roughness that traditional seals cannot offer an enough bridge. Biological studies done by Wainwright et al. (2013) on the adhesion of the northern clingfish has demonstrated that Contact Conformity, the ability of a soft interface to elastically adapt to microscopic surface asperities, will be the prerequisite for a practical seal on rough textures. Yet, it also introduces an engineering conflict: while low-modulus elastomers ensure an initial airtight interface, they are susceptible to failure under load tests. As Creton and Ciccotti (2016) demonstrate, soft polymers under a constant Static Load undergo elastic deformation. Over time, this creep causes the material to slowly pull away from the rough surface, resulting in micro-channel formation and vacuum failure.

A Possible Solution: Hybrid Interfaces 
To mitigate leakage on porous walls, common solutions rely on energy-intensive active pumps. Moving away from this, the current robotic research focuses on Hybrid material with similar usage. Takahashi et al. (2023) developed a soft hybrid suction cup utilizing an Adhesive Gel that can fill substrate pores, successfully generating 60 N of shear force on porous materials. Simultaneously, Chen et al. (2024) introduced a Rigid-Soft Hybrid architecture to address the creep and deformation problem of soft materials by proving that a rigid internal skeleton resists torque deformation significantly better than pure silicone. While these studies suggest that composite structures are mandatory to deal with the creep and deformation problem, these innovations are designed for robotic pick-and-place tasks. A limitation is remain for domestic vacuum-based installation, which will be a main key for the practical solution.

The Gap 
What remains missing is a longitudinal analysis of vacuum longevity specifically for furniture-scale loads. While recent studies evaluate the 'long-term' stability of hybrid seals, these testing horizons are typically constrained to short robotic operation testing cycles (Chen et al., 2024; Takahashi et al., 2023). There remains a critical gap in investigating the deformation and performance thresholds of rigid-soft hybrid interfaces under high-capacity continuous shear stress over the multi-month durations required for domestic furniture."
