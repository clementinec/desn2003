Research Question: How do pity systems and limited time rewards shape the decision
making process and post-pull satisfaction of adult gacha players in Mobile RPG games?

The concept of Pity Systems
Pity systems within Gacha games have been popularised through the release of a sub-section
of pity systems, as seen within Genshin’s 50/50. Due to the popularity, other modern games
have adopted the pity system with Zheng et al. indicating a 60% prevalence of a pity system,
within 20 top earning games (2025). Furthermore, a mathematical model conducted by
Thavamuni et al. with reference to Game Refinement Theory, suggests that the critical reward
system is around N~55, where the pity in Genshin is akin to the odds of gambling in
traditional slot machines (2025). These papers are in conjunction with the identification of the
gambling mechanics, whilst Tharvamuni et al. discuss the mathematical model and the final
consideration for players regarding how pity systems are priced to obtain the desired
outcome.

Artificial Scarcity of Limited Timed Rewards
The idea of limited timed rewards forms an artificial scarcity within the user. This can lead to
them making impulse decisions, where in a paper by Gibson et al (2023), discusses that users
were prompted to make a decision, based on the idea that the reward was time limited.
Furthermore with investigation regarding post-pull satisfaction, according to Hietamäki
(2023), around 80.2% of players did not regret their purchase after. When questioned, the
users suggested that the reason for purchasing was the act of obtaining a character or a
weapon. This leads to a precedent of participants who were willing to spend to obtain their
desired outcome.

However, there has been a conflict within the research, where in the findings found by
Ageenkova, 2025, there is a discussion of urgency mechanics, appearing for most users, and
making them feel annoyed. Yet, this research discusses other games, not limited to the scope
of this research. This research can be essential to understand how users can be pushed. The
research also discusses how participants have felt on the negative side of post-spend
satisfaction, with most participants identifying that they feel less inclined to spend if they are
not able to see the value.

Gap for Research?
The literature has two divided perspectives with the post-pull satisfaction. Hietamäki (2023),
mentions a 80.2% of participants not regretting, whereas Ageenkova discusses the regret in
other live service games. There can be further surveys conducted to investigate the user
sentiment.
