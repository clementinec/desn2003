Ski tourism has nowadays emerged as a significant global industry, yet the

complexity of destination choice remains challenging for travelers. This review visits

academic research on skier decision-making and evaluates how existing digital tools

address (or fail to address) these factors, revealing a critical disconnect between the

sophisticated customer psychology and the filtering algorithms current ski platforms

adopt.


Skier’s Decision Making Patterns


Studies discovered that ski destination choice is a multi-layered cognitive process.

Klenosky's research highlighted a means-end chain theory indicating that skiers tend

to choose destinations based not solely on the attributes themselves but on how

attributes incorporate experiential consequences that enhance personal experience

(Klenosky, Gengler, and Mulvey 362-379; Sun et al.). Push-pull research on skiers'

behaviors conducted by He and Luo also confirms that motivation structures affect

satisfaction and revisit intention, with pull factors such as facilities and convenience

exerting stronger influence than push factors such as personal intentions to ski (He

and Luo).


Another notable finding across studies is skier heterogeneity. Xu et al. show that the

attribute's importance varies dramatically throughout different resort types and user

segments. As the Chinese market is characterized by 80% beginners (Vanat), it

tends to prioritize accessibility and instruction, while enthusiasts in the European

market focus more on snow quality and trail diversity (Sun et al.). Critically, Xu et al.

reveal that attribute effects are often non-linear, while conventional recommender

systems often adapt attribute scores to be positively linear with user satisfaction,

which can lead to suboptimal recommendations for users with varying preferences

and needs.
Current Ski Platforms and Recommenders


General travel platforms mostly approach ski resort recommendations through

broad, multi-purpose frameworks that filter attributes in similar manners to hotels.

TripAdvisor's ski recommendations derive from its main popularity ranking algorithm,

which takes into account the quality, quantity, recency, and consistency of reviews

("How Tripadvisor Works"). While offering destination filters and AI semantic search,

its fundamental unit remains the traveler review and ranking rather than ski-specific

attributes. This yields several limitations that flattened the ski attributes into hotel

selections, where review-based rankings cover multiple satisfaction dimensions

while on-mountain conditions receive secondary attention and standardized ski

metrics are absent.


Ski specialized platforms, on the other hand, offer more relevant functionality but

remain limited in personalization. OnTheSnow serves as a real-time database

prioritizing snowfall, snow depth, open trails, and lift status ("About Us"). Its filters

enable matching by terrain composition, vertical drop, and ticket prices. However,

these functions remain generic scoring instead of personalized recommendations

that match the audience to their desired resort. Snomad represents the closest

existing recommender to personalized matching, containing filters across multiple

dimensions including runs, normal and off-piste terrain, lifts, and ski facilities ("Resort

Finder"). Users prioritize dimensions via a quiz interface generating top-three

matching recommendations. Personalization still remains filter-based. Its system

neither learns from behavior nor infers deeper needs, the 5-minute quick quiz lacks

comprehensive information about the audiences' preferences and expectations,

often resulting in mismatches.


Research Gap
Therefore, synthesizing across the reviewed literature and the current ski platforms,

a fundamental disconnect emerges between academic findings on skiers'

decision-making and the matching algorithms of existing digital tools.


Research has established destination choice as value-driven decision-making, with

means-end chains linking attributes to personal preferences (Klenosky, Gengler, and

Mulvey 362-379; Sun et al.), non-linear attribute effects across different thresholds

(Xu et al.), and motivation structures differentially affecting the satisfaction of visitors'

ski experiences (He and Luo). Although existing tools operate on different

approaches, none of the systems successfully track skiers' behavioral traits

highlighted in the research or infer deeper, heterogeneous needs. A

knowledge-based recommendation system—ideal for complex, value-driven,

multi-attribute domains—remains unapplied to ski recommenders on the market.
