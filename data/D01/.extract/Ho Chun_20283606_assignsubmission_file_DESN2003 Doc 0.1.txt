Literature review:

The idea that spatial design shapes human behavior isn’t new, in fact, it has been a
fundamental aspect of urban planning and architecture. Lynch's “The Image of the City”
(1960) established that people navigate built environments through cognitive mapping,
organizing space around five elements: paths, edges, districts, nodes, and landmarks. Hillier
and Hanson (1984) reinforced this concept further with the “space syntax theory”, which
analyzes how the configuration of space predicts patterns of movement and social encounter
in buildings and cities. These frameworks were developed for physical environments, but
their underlying logic, that spatial structure produces behavioral outcomes can be
implemented into game design.

One branch of research has approached game environments through recurring design
patterns and their intended gameplay functions. Hullett and Whitehead (2010) categorized
spatial patterns in first-person shooters, such as sniper positions, choke points, and open
areas, and argued that each pattern creates recognizable gameplay dynamics. However,
their analysis remained purely conceptual, and their paper only documented which types of
spatial arrangements existed in video game maps and their intended purpose, rather than
how players actually respond to them behaviorally and emotionally.

While more practical frameworks exist for map design in games, they cater mainly towards
RPGs with more significant narrative elements than FPS shooters. For example, Jenkins
(2004) offered a broader conceptual framework with his idea of "narrative architecture,"
proposing that game spaces communicate story through environmental arrangement rather
than exposition. His four categories, evocative, enacted, embedded, and emergent narrative
spaces, have been widely adopted across game studies. Yet Jenkins's study focuses solely
on spatial storytelling rather than the correlation between a virtual space's architectural
properties and the player's gameplay behavior. In his research, a narrow corridor funneling
the player toward a planned encounter is analyzed for what it communicates in terms of
storytelling, not for how its proportions, sightlines, or verticality influence the player's tactical
decisions and movement speed

On the player side, research has leaned heavily on psychological frameworks developed
outside game studies. Csikszentmihalyi's (1990) “theory of flow”, a state of optimal
engagement arising from a balance between challenge and skill, has been the dominant
lens, formalised for games by Sweetser and Wyeth (2005) in their GameFlow model.
GameFlow operationalized “flow” into a set of evaluative criteria for player enjoyment, and it
has been widely used. However, both flow theory and GameFlow treat the spatial
environment as background. Difficulty curves, pacing, and feedback loops are analyzed in
terms of mechanics and challenge design, not in terms of the physical architecture of the
space in which those mechanics unfold. A player may experience flow while moving through
a game map, but whether the map's ceiling height, spatiality, or architecture placement
contributed to or undermined that state has not been measured within this framework.

In contrast, a separate strand of research has approached the problem from the opposite
direction, not asking how spaces should be designed, but observing how players actually
behave within them. Drachen and Canossa (2009) demonstrated that geographic
information systems (GIS) could be applied to player movement data, producing heatmaps
and trajectory analyses that revealed how players actually navigated game worlds as
opposed to how designers intended them to. Their work showed that players frequently
ignored intended pathways and clustered in unexpected areas, suggesting a disconnect
between designer intent and spatial affordance.

Additionally, the rise of game statistics and analytics has opened new possibilities for
studying player-environment interaction. The work collected in El-Nasr, Drachen, and
Canossa's (2013) Game Analytics demonstrated that spatial data collected from player
sessions, such as movement traces and kill/death locations, could be systematically
visualized and analyzed, revealing behavioral patterns that clearly correlate with map
features. Players tend to engage through certain routes, avoid certain areas, and die
repeatedly at specific spatial junctions. These insights are useful for iterative design, but the
approach is fundamentally descriptive rather than explanatory. It reveals that behavior varies
across different spaces within the map without identifying which architectural elements are
responsible or why they produce the effects they do. A heatmap can show that players die
frequently at a particular junction, but it cannot provide an analysis on whether the cause is a
line-of-sight problem, an issue of spatial legibility, or something else entirely.


The Gap:
A knowledge gap identified across the reviewed literature is a lack of work that
systematically connects the architectural properties of game map environments, spatial
configuration, verticality, and line-of-sight to measured player gameplay behavior and
psychological response. The existing research provides architectural theory applied
conceptually to games (Nitsche, 2008; Totten, 2014), categorization of design patterns
organized around designer intent (Hullett & Whitehead, 2010), narrative frameworks for
interpreting game spaces (Jenkins, 2004), and analytic methodologies for tracking where
players go and what happens to them (El-Nasr et al., 2013). These are substantial
contributions, but they have not been synthesized into empirical studies that manipulate or
systematically compare architectural variables and measure their effects on how players
behave and how they experience play.

This gap is important to the industry because map architecture is one of the few design
elements that shape every moment of active gameplay. Compared to other game elements,
such as narrative, the spatial environment is continuously present, influencing decisions,
constraining movement, and structuring the player's decision at every instant. If specific
architectural properties have measurable and consistent effects on player behavior and
experience, then understanding those effects would provide AI models with an empirical
foundation for decisions that are currently guided by intuition and playtesting without clear
analytical frameworks for interpreting what the data means.
