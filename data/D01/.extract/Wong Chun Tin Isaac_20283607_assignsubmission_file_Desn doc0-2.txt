Consumption and the postsumer

Much of contemporary research on social media and consumption begins with the premise that digital
platforms intensifies consumer engagement by accelerating information flows, amplifying trends, and
personalising advertising. Marketing and communication studies consistently shows that exposure to
influencers and peer-generated content shows direct increases in purchase intentions and impulse buying
(Lou and Yuan, 2019). Social media platforms reduce search costs, normalize rapid trend cycles, and
integrate consumption patterns into our daily interactions. These body of works have been remarkably
influential in establishing a link between digital participation and purchasing behavior.

Over the last decade, scholars have begun to recognise the limitations of analysing social media effects
solely in terms of purchase intent. Research on “Instagrammable” consumption and visual culture
suggests that platforms do not merely advertise products but restructure goods as communicative devices
embedded in attention economies. (Marwick, 2015). Algorithmic systems prioritize visually distinctive and
circulation-ready content, rewarding aesthetic novelty and rapid turnover (van Dijck, Poell, and de Waal,
2018). Further empirical studies within the fashion industry show direct correlation between social media
engagement with more frequent purchasing and shorter trend cycles (McNeill and Venter, 2019). These
works shifts docus from discrete purchasing decisions to the temporal acceleration of consumption.

However, much of existing literature ends at acquisition. The focus remains on the analyzation of buying
frequency, brand engagement, and symbolic display, while consequences for product retention and
disposal are rarely examined. Social media participation is treated as a driver of demand rather than a
structural factor in material throughput, and the environmental implications of intensified
platform-mediated consumption remain largely unchecked.

Social media and identity

As stated previously, parallel scholarships in digital-platform sociology highlights the centrality of identity
within mediated-platforms. Marwick (2015) argues that social media users curate identities for networked
audiences while while van Dijck, Poell, and de Waal (2018) show how platform architectures prioritize
visibility of circulation-ready aesthetics. By participating in these social-platforms, it requires continual
acquisition and display of culturally legible content, or artifacts.

Gen Z , a population that experienced upbringing where social media is heavily embedded in daily life,
holds a particularly prominent position within this environment. Empirical research consistently shows
Gen Z cohorts exhibit greater integration of digital communication in their social loves, and have
heightened responsiveness to influencer culture (Djafarova and Bowes, 2021). This does not imply
intrinsic generational characteristics, rather a stronger structural immersion to platform-rich environments.
As a result, collecting practices in this cohort are likely to be more influenced by digital display logics than
previous generations.

The interconnectivity built into a social-platforms also amplifies social comparison between peers.
Conspicuous consumption is further embedded in algorithmically curated feeds, where peer possessions
are constantly visible. Exposure to upward comparison targets significantly reduces self-esteem, and
increases social comparison tendencies (Vogel et al., 2014). In such environments, goods must be
continuously produced as visual markers or hierarchy.

Despite this, research on collecting practices rarely links digital identity to material turnover. Fandom,
merchandise consumption and hobby culture studies tend to focus on community formation and symbolic
meaning, but seldom measure acquisition frequency, retention and disposal patterns. The link between
digital-display and waste outcomes remains untested.

Environmental Consequences of overconsumption

Research on environmental sociology have consistently demonstrated correlation in product throughput,
waste generation and shortened product cycles. Intensifying decline in product durability shows a
paralleled increase in resource extraction and waste generation (Cooper, 2005), while landfill pressure is
also directly tied to waste accumulation through everyday disposal practices (Evans, 2011).

Empirical research on modern day fast fashion provides clear evidence that accelerated trend cycles and
low retention periods significantly increases textile waste and systemic material accumulation (Niinimäki
et al., 2020). Similar patterns can be observed in Plastic-intensive industries such as toy and
merchandise sectors, where trend-driven turnover contributes to significant waste streams.

Yet, these works primarily analyses the production systems and regulatory frameworks, while consumer
behaviour is treated as secondary or background variables rather than the central explanatory
mechanism. Although shortened retention and increased acquisition are acknowledged, much of this
research was conducted prior to social media became structurally embedded within consumption
practices. As a result, environmental literature documents the consequences of turnover but does not
account how platform-mediated environments may amplify said turnover.

The Gap

Across the stated literatures, developments are evident, yet their insights have developed largely in
parallel. No integrated empirical study has directly tested whether participation in social-platforms have
correlations with increased material waste in collecting practices. This study address this gap by linking
social media participation to measurable indicators of material waste, acquisition frequency, retention
duration and disposal behaviour within Gen Z collecting practices and discovering whether participation in
social media platforms is systematically associated with increased material waste in collecting practices,
and how platform-mediated visibility may operationally accelerate acquisition and retention cycles within
Generation Z.
