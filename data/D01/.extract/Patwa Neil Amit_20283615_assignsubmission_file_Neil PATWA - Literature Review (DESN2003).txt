Title: Identifying the Gap in Fitness Tracking Applications
Name: Patwa Neil Amit
UID: 3036320688

The Foundation of Self-Tracking Fitness
Modern research into fitness applications can be incorporated into the Technology Acceptance Model
(TAM), which states that a user’s intention to use an app is primarily driven by their perspective of
usefulness and ease of use. Within the aspect of fitness and nutrition tracking, MyFitnessPal is a popular
application which has been the industry’s benchmark for a while now. Allowing to keep track of workout
routines and its productivity within many specific categories, and having an extensive food database, now
exceeding 14 million items, this app supports individuals by simplifying fitness tracking and the complex
task of caloric monitoring into a manageable daily habit.

However, app usage does not guarantee long-term adherence. While MyFitnessPal effectively allows
physiological tracking, it simultaneously operates on a deficit-based model which triggers what
researchers call “persuasive guilt”. A study led by University College London (UCL) noted that based on
user interviews, when users fail to meet daily numerical goals, the app’s feedback tends to transition from
a tool of motivation to a source of anxiety, resulting in discontinuing usage to prevent negative emotional
reinforcement (Greaves, 2025).

Personalization vs. Algorithmic Overload
Conversely, the evolution of Fitbit demonstrates a shift from simple-step counting to a wellness
ecosystem. Fitbit’s incorporation of heart rate variability, sleep stages and the provision of overall “Daily
Readiness Scores” reflects a step towards deep personalization. Unlike the manual concept of fitness
tracker applications, Fitbit uses sensors which automatically generates data collection, theoretically
reducing the “friction points” that’s known to lead to user fatigue.

Yet, this concept has introduced a new challenge; data visualization vs. data comprehension. While Fitbit
provides high-fidelity charts, a research study by Lepler, a student from Jonkoping University studying
Computer Science and Informatics suggests that an overload of physiological data without enough
qualitative context can result in users feeling disconnected (Lepler, 2023). The overload and complexity
of advanced user interfaces can decrease engagement. In this case, the “failed user” phenomenon is not
due to the lack of data, but rather an inability to conveniently convert the data into earned milestones.

The Gap
What remains missing is the framework that successfully fills the gap between physiological
quantification and psychological maintenance. Existing literature tends to label fitness tracking
applications as either “tools for the motivated” or “toys for the curious”. However, there’s a distinct lack
of research into the “disengaged user” within specific demographics, for example, the individuals who
start highly motivated but tend to feel that these apps under-address their psychological needs for
entertainment and variety. By focusing on these “friction points” where numerical goals clash with the
user’s lived experiences, further research can identify how to transform short-term tracking into long-term
tracking and beneficial habit formations necessary for true well-being, attempting to bridge the gap in the
productivity of fitness tracking applications.
