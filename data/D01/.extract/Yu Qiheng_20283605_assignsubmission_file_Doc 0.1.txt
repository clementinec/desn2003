                  The Social Dynamics of Sustainable Behavior Change

Research on why people act or fail to act in environmentally responsible ways has
generated rich and diverse results of scholarships. This review surveys three distinct
but related conversations: individualistic models of behavior change, sociological
accounts of practice and norms, and research on environmental communication. By
analyzing these literatures one by one, I identify an existing blind spot: the everyday
interpersonal interactions through which environmental meanings are negotiated and
behaviors are shaped.

Individual Models and Their Limits

The primary surveys in environmental psychology treat behavior as a function of
individual cognition. Ajzen's (1991) Theory of Planned Behavior proposes that
behavioral intention is predicted by attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived
behavioral control and widely applied in sustainability research. Studies have used
this framework to explain recycling, energy conservation, and transportation choices
(Bamberg & Möser, 2007). Stern and colleagues' (1999) Value-Belief-Norm theory
traces pro-environmental action through a causal chain from personal values to
awareness of consequences to a sense of responsibility to act.

These models have proven useful but face significant limitations. The persistent
disconnect between what people say and what they do remains poorly explained
within individualistic frameworks (Kollmuss & Agyeman, 2002). Such models
overemphasize individual choice while overlooking the social and material contexts
that shape what people can do. From this perspective, focusing on attitudes misses the
extent to which behavior is embedded in shared routines and infrastructures.

The Turn Toward Social and Normative Perspectives

The second literature category has responded by stressing social context. Cialdini and
colleagues' (1990) focus theory of normative conduct distinguished between
descriptive norms (what most people do) and injunctive norms (what most people
approve of). Field experiments demonstrated that simply informing people about
others' behavior. For instance, that most hotel guests reuse their towels can shift action
(Goldstein, Cialdini, & Griskevicius, 2008).

Subsequent research has refined this insight. Terry and Hogg (1996) found that
normative messages are more influential when they come from groups we identify
with. Peer effects have emerged in solar panel adoption (Bollinger & Gillingham,
2012) and household energy use (Peschiera & Taylor, 2012).

This kind of literature typically treats social influence as a kind of statistical force. It

tells us that networks matter but contains less about what happens when people talk.
The content of conversation, the quality of interaction, the emotional dynamics of
influence remain largely unexamined.

Social practice theory offers a more fundamental departure. Shove, Pantzar, and
Watson (2010) argue that the proper unit of analysis is not the individual but the
practice itself. From this perspective, behaviors like showering or heating a home are
not choices but performances of socially shared routines. The limitation for present
purposes is that practice theory focuses on the reproduction of practices across time
but has less to say about moments of change and the interpersonal dynamics of
transition.

Environmental Communication and the Interpersonal Realm

The third relevant literature category concerns how environmental issues are
communicated. Much of this work focuses on media framing and campaign
effectiveness (Nisbet, 2009). But a smaller body of research has examined
interpersonal communication specifically. Leiserowitz and colleagues (2016) found
that Americans rarely discuss climate change with friends and family, a silence they
argue inhibits engagement.

More recently, Geiger and Swim (2016) explored how conversations about climate
change can reduce polarization, suggesting that the quality of dialogue matters. But
this work remains nascent. We know that talking is important, but we lack systematic
understanding of what makes some conversations transformative while others go
nowhere.

Synthesis and the Identified Gap

Synthesizing these literatures reveals a clear pattern. Individualistic models tell us
much about internal determinants of behavior but little about social process. Norms
research and network studies prove that social context matters but treat interaction as
a black box. Practice theory embeds behavior in social routines but offers limited
tools for understanding change. Environmental communication research attends to
talk but has focused predominantly on mediated or public discourse.

What's missing is systematic attention to the interpersonal middle ground: the
specific, situated exchanges through which individuals try out new practices, seek
validation, negotiate meaning, and experience support or resistance. We have
fragments but no integrated account.
