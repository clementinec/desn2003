DESN2003 Doc 0.1 — Literature Review Assessment

Assessment Date: 2026-03-17 Assessor: Dr. Hongshan Guo (with AI-assisted initial review) Grading: 50% effort + 50% quality, curved to median 85%, min 75%, max 96%

Cohort Summary

15 submissions received. Overall, the cohort shows a wide spread in engagement level. The strongest submissions demonstrate genuine thematic synthesis, critical evaluation, and a clear narrative arc building toward a research gap. The weakest submissions are drastically under-length — one is barely a paragraph — while several others misunderstood the assignment brief or submitted fewer than 5 sources.

Common strengths across the cohort: - Most students identified a clear research topic and attempted to articulate a gap - Several students showed good critical thinking about methodology, not just content - Source selection was generally relevant to stated research questions

Common weaknesses across the cohort: - Many reviews still read as paper-by-paper summaries rather than thematic synthesis - Source counts frequently fall short of the 8–12 source guideline — fewer than half the cohort meets it - Gap statements tend to be vague or underdeveloped — often just 1–2 sentences - Critical evaluation of sources (limitations, methods, generalizability) is consistently light

Flags: - Ho Chun & Chan Yuen Yau Davina: Agent flagged as potentially identical, but evaluations describe completely different topics (FPS game architecture vs. passive building design). Recommend manual verification — likely a false flag from the combined “Doc 0 + Doc 0.1” file format.

Individual Evaluations

1. Li Ping Sum — Curved: 96

Topic: FOMO and nostalgia-based emotional branding strategies driving emotional consumption across Generation Z

Quality: 44/50 | Effort: 46/50 | Raw: 90

What you did well: - This is a properly built literature review. The structure — moving from defining emotional consumption, to Gen Z characteristics, to FOMO and nostalgia as distinct drivers, to brand strategy across four industry categories, to a clear research gap — shows you understand what a lit review is supposed to do. - Excellent source range: approximately 28–30 references spanning academic journals (Richins, Przybylski, Wildschut, Elhai), industry reports (BCG, McKinsey/BOF), and trade sources. Far exceeds the 8–12 target. - The thematic synthesis is genuinely strong. The FOMO section, for example, integrates Przybylski’s psychological framework with Elhai’s reinforcement cycle and Argan’s consumption model to build a layered argument. That’s exactly how synthesis should work.

Where to improve: - Some definitional sources are lightweight (e.g., a marketing blog for “emotional consumption,” MBA Skool dictionary for “hedonic consumption”). For core definitions, peer-reviewed sources carry more weight. - The brand strategy section (2.4) reads more like a market landscape scan than a critical review — it describes what brands do without evaluating evidence of whether these strategies actually work. - At least two citations appear mismatched: Nations (2022) links to a marine litter report, and Doob (1950) is about Goebbels’ propaganda, not Uses & Gratifications theory. Audit your reference list carefully.

For Doc 1: Fix the citation errors, replace lightweight definitional sources with peer-reviewed equivalents, and add critical evaluation to the brand strategy section.

2. Yu Qiheng — Curved: 93

Topic: The Social Dynamics of Sustainable Behavior Change

Quality: 42/50 | Effort: 42/50 | Raw: 84

What you did well: - Excellent thematic structure organized around three distinct literature conversations (individual behavioral models, social norms and practice theory, environmental communication) rather than paper-by-paper. This is a textbook example of synthetic organization. - The gap identification is sharp and genuinely earned: each section builds toward the “interpersonal middle ground” that none of the three literatures adequately covers. The narrative arc is clear and persuasive. - Sources are high quality (Ajzen, Cialdini, Shove, Stern, Kollmuss & Agyeman) and span foundational theory through recent empirical work. 13 references with proper DOIs is solid.

Where to improve: - The review relies heavily on canonical, well-known sources. It would benefit from more recent or surprising findings that complicate the neat three-stream narrative. - Critical evaluation of individual studies is light — the review identifies limitations of each stream but rarely interrogates the methods or findings of specific papers. - The newest cited work is from 2016. Add 2–3 post-2020 sources that begin to address the interpersonal gap you’ve identified.

For Doc 1: Add recent sources, include at least one paragraph of methodological critique, and push the gap statement further into what specifically your research will investigate.

3. Ho Chun — Curved: 91

Topic: Architectural elements in FPS game maps and player positioning/playstyle (Counter-Strike 2)

Quality: 40/50 | Effort: 40/50 | Raw: 80

What you did well: - Excellent synthesis across multiple research traditions — urban planning theory (Lynch, Hillier & Hanson), game design patterns (Hullett & Whitehead), narrative architecture (Jenkins), player psychology (Csikszentmihalyi), and spatial analytics (Drachen & Canossa). These are woven into a coherent argument, not just listed. - Strong critical evaluation: each source is assessed for what it fails to do — Hullett & Whitehead are “purely conceptual,” Jenkins focuses on storytelling not behavior, heatmaps are “descriptive rather than explanatory.” - The gap is precise: no existing work systematically connects architectural properties of game maps to measured player behavior.

Where to improve: - The submission includes substantial Doc 0 material (pages 1–2) that wasn’t required for Doc 0.1. The actual literature review portion is only about 2.5 pages. - Source count is adequate (10 references) but leans on older foundational texts (Lynch 1960, Csikszentmihalyi 1990). Add recent empirical work on game environment-player interaction. - ⚠ Potential flag: verify this submission against Chan Yuen Yau Davina’s — initial automated check suggested overlap, though the topics appear different. Manual review recommended.

For Doc 1: Remove the Doc 0 material, add 2–3 recent empirical sources, and expand the gap section.

4. Wong Chun Tin Isaac — Curved: 90

Topic: Platform-mediated waste generation in Gen Z collecting practices

Quality: 40/50 | Effort: 38/50 | Raw: 78

What you did well: - The strongest structural design in the batch. Three clear thematic streams (consumption/platforms, identity/social comparison, environmental consequences) with the gap emerging naturally from showing these streams developed “largely in parallel” without integration. That’s exactly how a literature review should work. - Source selection is strong: Niinimaki et al. (2020) in Nature Reviews Earth & Environment, van Dijck et al. (2018) from Oxford UP, Marwick (2015) in Public Culture. These are high-quality, well-cited works. - Genuine and specific critical evaluation — environmental literature “primarily analyses production systems” while treating consumer behavior as secondary; consumption research “ends at acquisition” without examining disposal.

Where to improve: - Some sources are reused across sections (Marwick, van Dijck), reducing the breadth. A few more sources specifically on collecting practices or fandom studies would help. - Minor grammatical errors throughout (“shifts docus,” “social loves” for “social lives”). The intellectual quality deserves a proofreading pass. - Collecting-specific literature is notably thin — consider Belk’s work on possessions and the extended self, or Pearce on collecting.

For Doc 1: Add collecting-specific sources, proofread carefully, and ensure each thematic stream has distinct sources.

Sources are genuinely woven together rather than listed sequentially. - Multiple clearly identified gaps (need for comprehensive research, need for bookstore-specific analysis, need for Hong Kong context) that build a convincing case for the project. - Good range of 9 credible academic sources spanning environmental design, marketing, and service quality, with effective use of direct quotations.

For Doc 1: Add sources that directly study Hong Kong bookstores or HK retail culture, and strengthen the synthesis in the intangible section by organizing around themes rather than individual papers.

6. Bai Junyi — Curved: 89

Topic: “Ugly aesthetics” in blind box character design and university students’ consumption behavior

Quality: 38/50 | Effort: 38/50 | Raw: 76

What you did well: - Well-organized thematic structure that moves logically from aesthetic paradigm shifts to blind box market context to the research gap. This reads as a genuine narrative, not a source dump. - Good theoretical grounding: Baudrillard’s symbolic consumption, Birmingham School’s resistance consumption, Lorenz’s Baby Schema are integrated naturally into the argument. - The research gap is clearly and specifically identified: we know “ugly aesthetics” sells, but we don’t understand the micro-level psychological mechanism for university students, and why their liminal life stage matters.

Where to improve: - Source count is on the lower end — roughly 6–7 distinct sources, below the 8–12 expected range. - Some citations are under-developed: Hebdige (1979) on “resistance consumption” is mentioned but never unpacked or critiqued. - The review could go deeper on consumption psychology — more on how symbolic value has been studied empirically in similar product categories.

For Doc 1: Add 3–4 more sources (especially empirical studies on collectible consumption among young adults), and spend more time critically evaluating the theories cited.

7. Wong Ho Yan — Curved: 86

Topic: Design features of pop culture collectibles and emotional consumption in HK Gen Z

Quality: 36/50 | Effort: 34/50 | Raw: 70

What you did well: - Clear argumentative structure: identifies that existing research over-relies on questionnaires/metrics, shows that physical dimensions of collectible engagement are under-studied, and builds to a gap about design features that enable emotional value. - Good critical thinking about methodology — using Becker (2018) to critique questionnaire dominance shows sophistication. - The gap is well-articulated: the distinction between blind-box mechanisms and the broader category of Trendy Toys and Collectibles is specific and actionable.

Where to improve: - Several sources are from lower-impact venues (PARADE RISET, Journal of Undergraduate Social Science and Technology). Anchor the argument in more established consumer psychology or design research literature. - The review is narrowly scoped without connecting to broader theoretical frameworks (affect theory, object attachment, material culture studies) that could lend more weight. - Some cited sources (Andriyani 2025, Suprawan et al. 2024) barely appear in the body text. If a source is worth citing, it’s worth discussing.

For Doc 1: Connect to established frameworks (Norman’s emotional design, Csikszentmihalyi’s meaning of things, Belk’s extended self), and engage more deeply with each cited source.

8. Yu Yunqi — Curved: 85

Topic: Ski tourism destination choice and digital recommendation systems

Quality: 35/50 | Effort: 34/50 | Raw: 69

What you did well: - The two-part structure (academic research on skier decision-making vs. analysis of existing platforms) is a smart, practical framing that directly sets up the applied research gap. - Genuine critical engagement with existing platforms — TripAdvisor, OnTheSnow, and Snomad are each evaluated against academic findings, not just described. The observation that TripAdvisor “flattens ski attributes into hotel selections” is sharp. - The gap is clearly articulated: disconnect between value-driven decision-making literature and filter-based platform design, with the specific proposal for a knowledge-based recommendation system.

Where to improve: - Only 8 sources total, and 3 are website pages rather than academic literature. The academic foundation is thin with only 5 scholarly sources. - Roughly 800 words of body text — this feels like a first pass rather than a thorough engagement with the literature. - No critical evaluation of the academic sources themselves — Klenosky’s study is from 1993 and the ski tourism landscape has changed dramatically since then.

For Doc 1: Expand academic literature significantly — add recommender system literature (content-based, collaborative filtering, knowledge-based) and UX research on travel tech. Engage critically with the age and context of the existing ski tourism studies.

9. Zhuang Victor — Curved: 84

Topic: Preservation of Jiaohe Ancient City (earthen heritage site conservation)

Quality: 32/50 | Effort: 33/50 | Raw: 65

What you did well: - Genuinely interesting and niche topic — a specific, under-studied problem (non-invasive preservation of earthen heritage sites) with domain-appropriate sources. - Honest self-reflection about the review’s limitations — noting the dominance of a single nationality of authors shows awareness of bias. - Decent thematic progression from urgency/context to current techniques to gap.

Where to improve: - Very short — only about 2 pages of body text. This is significantly under the expected depth for a literature review. - The review is narrowly focused on anchoring techniques. Broaden to cover digital documentation/monitoring (photogrammetry, LiDAR), environmental mitigation, and comparative approaches at other earthen sites worldwide. - The gap statement is vague and reads as a single run-on sentence. It needs to clearly state what is missing, why it matters, and what your research will specifically contribute.

10. Chan Yuen Yau Davina — Curved: 83

Topic: Passive design strategies, natural ventilation, and AI integration in building planning (Hong Kong context)

Quality: 28/50 | Effort: 32/50 | Raw: 60

What you did well: - Good range of sources (11 references) spanning academic papers, government data, industry publications, and technical reports — shows breadth of reading. - The thematic sections attempt to create a narrative arc from problem to gap, and include concrete Hong Kong context with specific data sources. - Includes practical thinking about feasibility (HK Observatory, government open data, Esri China).

Where to improve: - The writing is heavily summary-based rather than synthesized — almost every paragraph opens with “According to…” or “As stated in…” and presents one source at a time without comparing or contrasting. - Critical evaluation is mostly absent. Sources are reported as facts without questioning limitations, methodology, or applicability. - The “Bridge Between the Gap” section is vague and assertion-driven (“I believe the gap between…”) rather than precisely articulating what the literature has and hasn’t established.

For Doc 1: Restructure so each paragraph synthesizes multiple sources around a theme. Sharpen the gap: instead of “I believe the gap between traditional methods and AI,” specify exactly what has been studied vs. what has not.

Quality: 30/50 | Effort: 25/50 | Raw: 55

What you did well: - Clean thematic organization moving from the core problem to potential solutions to the remaining gap. - Sources are genuinely relevant and technical — Wainwright et al. on clingfish adhesion, Creton & Ciccotti on polymer creep, hybrid suction cup studies. This shows targeted reading. - The identified gap is specific and actionable: no one has tested hybrid seals under continuous furniture-scale loads over months.

Major areas are missing: domestic mounting solutions, material science of wall surfaces, consumer product standards. - No reference list provided. This is a basic formatting requirement.

12. Pradhan Arjun Kumar — Curved: 82

Topic: Gacha pity systems and limited-time rewards in mobile gaming

Quality: 28/50 | Effort: 22/50 | Raw: 50

What you did well: - The review engages with a niche but well-defined topic and identifies a genuine tension — Hietamaki’s 80.2% no-regret finding versus Ageenkova’s evidence of annoyance and regret. - Sources are credible and relevant: peer-reviewed journals and a King’s College London manuscript. 13. Patwa Neil Amit — Curved: 81

Topic: Fitness app design and user adoption (MyFitnessPal vs. Fitbit)

Quality: 25/50 | Effort: 20/50 | Raw: 45

What you did well: - The writing has a natural, engaging voice — phrases like “persuasive guilt” and “tools for the motivated vs. toys for the curious” show you’re genuinely thinking about the material. - There’s a clear narrative arc from MyFitnessPal’s tracking model to Fitbit’s sensor approach to the gap. - The critical evaluation of Fitbit’s data overload problem shows an ability to identify limitations in existing solutions.

Topic: Comparing Reddit’s anecdotal knowledge with fact-centric websites

15. Ye Lo A (Lucile) — Curved: 75

Topic: Understanding the interaction between social anxiety and appearance anxiety: Pathways and impacts on well-being

Quality: 8/50 | Effort: 5/50 | Raw: 13

What you did well: - The research question is well-framed and has genuine design-research potential — linking social anxiety and appearance anxiety to intervention/design opportunities is a good angle. - The four sources cited (Gao et al. 2023, Patel & Cougle 2024a, Patel & Cougle 2025, Wilver et al. 2020) are all peer-reviewed and directly relevant. You clearly found the right literature — you just didn’t engage with it.

Where to improve: - This submission is approximately 90 words of literature review content — a single paragraph. That is not a literature review. It is barely an abstract. - The paragraph only summarizes what the studies found. There is no synthesis (how do these studies relate to each other?), no critical evaluation (what are their limitations?), and no gap statement (what remains unknown?). - No reference list is provided. The in-text citations are the only indication of sources. - There is no structure: no thematic sections, no subheadings, no progression of argument.

For Doc 1: This needs to be built essentially from scratch. The topic and research question are fine — the problem is execution. You need to: (1) find at least 8–12 sources covering social anxiety, appearance anxiety, self-compassion interventions, body image in digital contexts, and design-for-wellbeing literature; (2) organize them thematically into 3–4 sections; (3) synthesize — don’t just list what each study found, but show how they connect, conflict, or leave gaps; (4) write a clear gap statement explaining what your research will address. Aim for at least 800–1000 words. Come to office hours if you need help getting started — the topic has real potential.
